# Atmosphere Guide - Last Shift

## Visual Tone

The facility should feel old, industrial, neglected, and recently abandoned. It should look functional enough to be believable, but damaged enough to feel unsafe.

## Lighting Rules

- Use darkness to create tension, not confusion.
- Always keep the next critical path readable.
- Use flickering lights sparingly.
- Use monitor glow and emergency lights as guidance.
- Red lighting should be saved for lockdown or warning moments.

## Color Direction

- Neutral concrete, metal, and office colors
- Sickly green or cool white fluorescents
- Warning yellow on industrial labels
- Red only for alarms, emergency signs, and lockdown

## Prop Usage

Props should explain what happened:
- Empty desks
- Abandoned tools
- Open lockers
- Broken wall panels
- Safety posters
- Evacuation notes
- Damaged equipment

Do not clutter rooms so much that navigation becomes unclear.

## Environmental Storytelling Rules

- Each room should communicate one idea.
- Notes and logs should be short.
- Props should support the main path.
- Important interactables should be visually distinct.
- Avoid long text dumps.

## Horror Hint Strategy

The monster or entity should mostly be suggested through:
- Sound
- Shadows
- Damage
- Logs
- Distant movement
- Locked doors
- Sudden facility responses

Do not show the monster directly in Level 1 unless absolutely necessary.

## What to Show

- Evidence of evacuation
- Damaged systems
- Unsafe old facility areas
- Warning messages
- Doors locking during the ending

## What to Hide

- Full monster appearance
- Complete explanation of the facility incident
- Exact source of sounds
- Too much backstory in Level 1

## Portfolio Goal

The atmosphere should show strong taste and restraint. A small level with clear lighting, good sound timing, and readable storytelling is the target.
